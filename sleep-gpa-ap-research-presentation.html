Question and Purpose

Why test sleep inside a lifestyle dataset?

  • Research question: To what extent is nightly sleep duration associated with GPA in the Student Lifestyle Dataset while considering other lifestyle factors?
  • Sleep matters because it supports attention, memory consolidation, and cognitive control.
  • Academic performance is shaped by competing daily behaviors, not one isolated habit.
  • The purpose was to test whether sleep still appears meaningful inside one open, downloadable file.
Framing Statement

If the dataset records a full 24-hour routine, does sleep still predict academic performance once other uses of time are considered?

Literature Overview
Prior Scholarship

Background / Context

Prior research treats sleep as credible, but not isolated.

  • Objective and longitudinal studies often connect longer, more consistent sleep with stronger academic outcomes.
  • GPA is also shaped by broader lifestyle patterns such as study habits, time use, and competing daily behaviors.
  • Several non-sleep lifestyle effects are modest or mixed, which makes context and measurement important.
  • Main takeaway: sleep matters, but its meaning depends on what variables are present and how they are measured.
Okano et al. 2019

Sleep quality, duration, and consistency aligned with better academic performance.

Creswell et al. 2023

Average nightly sleep early in the semester predicted end-of-term GPA.

Taylor et al. 2013

Sleep still mattered, but only when interpreted beside other academic predictors.

Park & Swanson 2021

Time-use patterns relate to GPA, yet many effects are relatively small.

06
Research Design
Methodology
Quantitative Design

Methodology

Secondary quantitative analysis of one public dataset.

Source 2,000 cases

Kaggle Student Lifestyle Dataset, 8 columns, no missing values retained.

Variables GPA + Sleep

GPA as the dependent variable; sleep as the main predictor.

Controls Study / Extra / Physical

Compared sleep against other plausible uses of time in the model.

Tests r, ANOVA, OLS

Used descriptive statistics, Pearson correlation, ANOVA, and regression.

Model Choice

Final model: GPA = b0 + b1(Sleep) + b2(Study) + b3(Extracurricular) + b4(Physical Activity) + e

Significance level: alpha = .05

Critical Design Note

The five time-use variables summed to exactly 24 hours in every observation. Social hours were omitted from the regression to avoid perfect multicollinearity, and stress was omitted because the dataset description says it is derived from study and sleep.

Analysis
What the Model Means

Analysis

Study time dominated the model; sleep still did not.

  • In the multivariable model, sleep was still not significant: B = -0.0059, p = .122.
  • Study hours were the strongest academic signal: r = .734 with GPA and B = 0.1531, p < .001 in regression.
  • The overall model fit was strong (R2 = .541), but that fit came mostly from study time.
  • Because the file encodes a closed 24-hour system, the coefficients reflect tradeoffs across the whole day.

Conclusion
Answer to the Question

Conclusion

Within this dataset, sleep duration did not meaningfully explain GPA.

Most Defensible Claim

The hypothesis was not supported by the Student Lifestyle Dataset.

Study time, not sleep time, emerged as the clearest predictor of academic performance in this file.

Sleep duration: weak / null Study hours: strongest signal Scope: this dataset only
Hypothesis Not Supported The observed file did not match the expected positive sleep pattern.
Best Predictor Study Time The strongest academic signal tracked hours allocated to studying.
Bounded Claim Context Matters The result explains this structured dataset, not all students everywhere.

Implications
Why It Matters

Implications

The result says as much about the dataset as it does about the topic.

Use

Open datasets are valuable because the analysis is transparent, accessible, and easy for another student to replicate.

Caution

Accessibility is not the same as representativeness. A simple file can still produce narrow or misleading inferences if its provenance is weak.

Topic-Level Lesson

Simplified duration-only measures can understate a complex behavior like sleep quality, consistency, and timing.

Research-Level Lesson

Student researchers should examine variable construction, tradeoffs, and documentation before generalizing findings.

Establish Argument
Limitations
Study Weaknesses

Limitations

The strongest limitations come from measurement and documentation.

01

No published sampling or recruitment procedure was provided for the dataset.

02

The design was cross-sectional, so the project can describe association but not causation.

03

Sleep was measured only as duration, not quality, regularity, timing, or fatigue.

04

The file lacked demographic and academic-background controls such as prior GPA or course load.

05

The closed 24-hour structure created compositional dependence among activities.

Future Research
Next Step Roadmap

Future Research

A stronger follow-up would improve both the data and the method.

01

Use datasets with documented instruments, clearer sampling, and stronger provenance.

02

Add richer sleep measures, especially quality, consistency, and timing.

03

Compare open secondary data with institutional or longitudinal cohorts.

04

Apply compositional-data methods to variables constrained to a fixed 24-hour day.

Data Better Documentation

A stronger file should explain how participants and variables were actually generated.

Sleep More Than Duration

Quality and regularity are prominent in the strongest published sleep studies.

Design Longitudinal Evidence

Repeated measurement would better match the literature's view of cumulative sleep effects.

Analysis Compositional Methods

These methods fit time-budget data better than treating all activities as independent.

The hardest part was recognizing how provenance and the 24-hour constraint limited interpretation.

I learned to treat multicollinearity, measurement quality, and documentation as part of the argument.

What I'd Change Stronger Evidence

If I continued this project, I would keep the topic but move to a better-documented, richer dataset.

Defense-Ready Takeaway

The new understanding is not simply that "sleep does not matter." It is that conclusions about student success depend on how a dataset defines student life, measures behavior, and structures tradeoffs.
